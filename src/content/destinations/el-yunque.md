## Why El Yunque

El Yunque is the only tropical rainforest in the entire US National Forest System. That alone makes it special. But what makes it unforgettable is the experience of hiking through 28,000 acres of dense, dripping jungle — past waterfalls you can swim in, through cloud forests where the trees are covered in moss, and under a canopy so thick the sunlight turns green.

The forest gets over 200 inches of rain per year in its highest elevations, feeding dozens of streams and waterfalls. It's home to the coquí, a tiny tree frog found nowhere else on Earth, whose distinctive "ko-KEE!" call fills the evening air. At just 45 minutes from San Juan, it's the easiest tropical rainforest in the world to access.

## Things to Do

**La Mina Falls** — The most popular trail. A 0.7-mile paved path descends through the forest to a 35-foot waterfall with a swimming hole at its base. Expect crowds by 11 AM — go early. The water is cold and refreshing.

**Mt. Britton Tower** — A 1.8-mile trail climbing to an observation tower at 3,075 feet. On clear mornings (go before 10 AM), the views extend across the entire northeastern coast. The trail is steep and usually muddy.

**Yokahú Tower** — No hiking required. Drive up and climb the spiral staircase for panoramic forest and ocean views. Great for photos and orientation.

**Juan Diego Falls** — Less crowded alternative to La Mina. A short trail through bamboo forest to a series of cascading pools perfect for swimming. Off Route 191 on the southern side.

**El Toro Trail** — The challenging option. 3.5 miles to the highest peak (3,533 feet) through cloud forest. You'll likely be in the clouds at the summit. Bring layers — it's 15°F cooler up top.

## Where to Eat

**Yuquiyú Delights** — Food truck near the forest entrance serving alcapurrias, empanadillas, and fresh-squeezed juice. Perfect fuel before or after a hike. $3-8.

**The Rainforest Inn** — Small inn and restaurant on the road up to El Yunque. Puerto Rican comfort food with a view. $12-20.

## Scott's Pro Tips

<div class="scott-tips">

**Weather** — It's a rainforest. It will rain. Bring a rain jacket and waterproof your phone. The rain usually comes in short bursts. Morning hours (7-10 AM) tend to be the driest and clearest.

**What to Bring** — Waterproof hiking shoes (trails are muddy), rain jacket, insect repellent, swimsuit (for waterfall pools), towel, 2 liters of water, and snacks.

**Reservations** — Book your entry time slot at recreation.gov. $2/person. Peak season fills up 2-3 weeks ahead. Weekday mornings are least crowded.

**Getting Around** — Only one road (Route 191) goes into the forest from the north. The southern access via Route 191 is still partially closed from Hurricane Maria. All main trailheads are accessible from the northern entrance.

**Wildlife** — The coquí frog is most active at dusk and dawn. The Puerto Rican parrot (critically endangered, only ~600 exist) lives here but sightings are rare. Look for green flashes between the trees.
